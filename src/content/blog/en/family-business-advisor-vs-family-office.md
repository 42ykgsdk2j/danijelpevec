<p>Founders building generational wealth tend to encounter two terms that sound similar and are often used interchangeably: family business advisor and family office. They are not the same thing. They serve different functions, have different cost structures, and apply at different stages of a family's evolution.</p>

<p>For a founder weighing what kind of professional support makes sense, understanding the difference is the first step in spending money — and attention — wisely.</p>

<h2>What a family business advisor does</h2>

<p>A family business advisor is a person — usually working independently or with a small team — who advises the founder and the family on the strategic and human questions that shape the business and its future.</p>

<p>Their work typically covers:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Succession planning</strong> — designing the multi-year transition from one generation to the next.</li>
<li><strong>Family governance</strong> — drafting family constitutions, establishing family councils, defining decision rights.</li>
<li><strong>Conflict surfacing and resolution</strong> — facilitating conversations the family cannot have on its own.</li>
<li><strong>Strategic decisions</strong> — the moments where business, family and long-term considerations intersect.</li>
<li><strong>Next-generation development</strong> — mentoring, structured exposure, and honest feedback that family members rarely receive internally.</li>
</ul>

<p>A family business advisor does not manage assets, hold custody of money, or execute transactions. They sit beside the founder and the family during the decisions that no spreadsheet can resolve.</p>

<h2>What a family office does</h2>

<p>A family office is an organization — sometimes a single person, more often a team of professionals — that manages a family's financial and personal affairs. The classic functions of a family office include:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Investment management</strong> — portfolio strategy, asset allocation, manager selection across public and private markets.</li>
<li><strong>Accounting and tax</strong> — consolidated reporting, tax planning, multi-jurisdiction compliance.</li>
<li><strong>Trust and estate administration</strong> — managing structures, distributions, beneficiary relationships.</li>
<li><strong>Concierge and lifestyle services</strong> — in larger family offices, anything from property management to travel coordination.</li>
<li><strong>Risk and insurance</strong> — coverage strategy across the family's assets and exposures.</li>
</ul>

<p>Family offices come in two main forms. A <strong>single-family office</strong> serves one family exclusively, with dedicated staff and infrastructure. A <strong>multi-family office</strong> serves several unrelated families, sharing infrastructure and reducing per-family cost.</p>

<h2>The functional difference, in plain terms</h2>

<p>The simplest way to think about the distinction:</p>

<ul>
<li>A <strong>family business advisor</strong> helps the family decide what to do.</li>
<li>A <strong>family office</strong> helps the family execute and manage what has been decided.</li>
</ul>

<p>The two are complementary, not substitutable. A founder with a strong family office but no advisor often finds themselves making the most consequential decisions — succession, governance, conflict — without anyone in the room whose role is to slow the conversation down. A founder with a strong advisor but no family office can find that even good decisions are poorly executed.</p>

<div class="pull-quote">An advisor helps the family decide what to do. A family office helps the family execute what has been decided.</div>

<h2>When each makes sense</h2>

<h3>When a family business advisor adds the most value</h3>

<p>An advisor becomes valuable as soon as the founder begins thinking about transition, succession, or any decision that touches the family as well as the business. The triggers are usually:</p>

<ul>
<li>The founder is starting to think about the next generation taking on responsibility.</li>
<li>Family conflict has surfaced and needs to be addressed structurally rather than situationally.</li>
<li>A material exit, sale or restructuring is being considered.</li>
<li>The family is approaching the limits of what informal governance can hold.</li>
</ul>

<p>Cost is typically a fraction of what a family office requires, because the work is concentrated in defined engagements rather than ongoing operational management.</p>

<h3>When a family office becomes economically viable</h3>

<p>A single-family office is a substantial undertaking. Below roughly $250 million in liquid wealth, the cost of building one rarely justifies the benefit. Above that threshold, the calculus shifts — the family has enough complexity, assets and recurring needs to support dedicated infrastructure.</p>

<p>Below the single-family-office threshold, families typically combine:</p>

<ul>
<li>An external <strong>family business advisor</strong> for strategic and family questions.</li>
<li>A <strong>multi-family office</strong> or wealth manager for investment and reporting needs.</li>
<li>Specialist firms (tax, legal, audit) on a project basis.</li>
</ul>

<p>This combination gives small and mid-sized families most of the benefit of a single-family office at a fraction of the cost.</p>

<h2>Common confusion and mistakes</h2>

<ul>
<li><strong>Building a family office before there is enough wealth or complexity to justify it.</strong> The infrastructure is expensive; the actual workload is often modest. Many early family offices struggle to justify their fixed cost.</li>
<li><strong>Asking the family office to do advisory work.</strong> Family office staff are often deeply competent at execution and operations, but the role is structurally constrained — they answer to the family. Independent perspective on family conflict requires independence.</li>
<li><strong>Asking an advisor to manage assets.</strong> Advisory work requires distance from the daily transactions. An advisor who is also managing the money has competing incentives at the moments they most need to be neutral.</li>
<li><strong>Treating the choice as either-or.</strong> The most resilient family wealth structures combine both functions, with each clearly scoped.</li>
</ul>

<h2>The decision framework</h2>

<p>For a founder asking which they need first, the simple version:</p>

<ol>
<li>If you are facing succession, governance, or family decisions that have no clean answer — start with an <strong>advisor</strong>.</li>
<li>If your investment portfolio has outgrown what an external wealth manager can sensibly handle — explore a <strong>multi-family office</strong> first, then a <strong>single-family office</strong> if scale supports it.</li>
<li>In most cases, a thoughtful family ends up with both — an advisor for the questions, a family office or external infrastructure for the execution.</li>
</ol>

<p>The order tends to be: advisor first, family office later, single-family office only when the scale clearly justifies it.</p>
